Gold

How Gold Funds the Struggle Against Military Rule

Gold mining has long been a lucrative source of income in Myanmar for both the government and non-state armed groups. But after the 2021 coup, conflict, a collapsing economy and powerful militias have triggered a new gold rush across parts of central and northern Myanmar.

Much of the mining takes place on creeks and rivers, or along their banks and in nearby fields. Minerals, including gold, are leached from Myanmar before the coup, with the Wa State Army lacking arms, a group regime militia, informally taxing the miners.

The alluvial-mining operations employ tens of thousands of people, mostly migrants from elsewhere in Myanmar. But the mining has also reshaped the landscape - destroying farmland, carving up riverbanks and in some places even changing the course of waterways.

Since the military takeover, miners have pushed south along the Chindwin River into areas where the regime has lost control. In some resistance-dominated areas, local administrations and armed groups signed with the National Unity Government have allowed gold dredging boats to operate, collecting annual licence fees and monthly taxes.

The money can be used to purchase arms, ammunition and equipment. The same gold rush is also bringing abuses and pressure on communities, often including miners. The waste is dumped back into the river.

The dredging has provoked protests from nearby communities. The sandbanks that emerge during the dry season are prized for their fertile soil, but once miners churn them up, they can no longer be cultivated. Residents also fear the mining will drive military attacks.

Resistance groups say taxing gold mines is one of the few ways they can pay weapons and ammunition. But some local leaders are also accused of profiting personally from the industry, including by running dredging boats themselves.

Local regime officials have also benefited, collecting bribes and informal taxes from operators. But after battlefield losses and a shake-up in Sagaing Region's security command in November 2024, the regime ended permits in some areas - again calling for stricter controls even as fighting around the mines continues.

Satellite imagery shows that the boom extends well beyond the Chindwin. Along the Mu River and its tributaries in northern Sagaing State, open-pit mining has filled in river courses and altered terrain since the coup. Around Thaphanseik Dam in Kyunhla Township, the mining footprint more than doubled between 2021 and 2025, reaching more than 3,900 hectares. Much of this mining has spread from Namaletkon into former rice paddy fields - once right up to the edge of houses. Farmers say armed groups pressure them to sell land or tolerate leaving communities with polluted water, damaged fields and little recourse.

Areas rich in underground gold mines have also become military objectives. In Saw and Yesagyo, Kachin Independence Army-backed resistance forces seized the town and nearby mining areas in September 2025 before the military and Burma Nationality Army counterattacked. The resistance later abandoned the town, but still controls many mines in the surrounding area.

Gold from the mining concession is easy to loot, place or resell to relatives in Mandalay. Local demand is high because of Kyat instability, capital controls and demand for safe-haven assets. But because gold can fetch higher prices in neighbouring India and Bangladesh, traders have strong incentive to smuggle it across Myanmar's porous borders.

Gold mining provides this in a decentralised economy and revenue for armed groups fighting the regime. But it also destroys farmland, pollutes waterways and places local communities under dangerous pressure. It widens targets in the war. For those living near the mines, the costs often outweigh the benefits. For armed groups and local commanders, however, gold has become too valuable to ignore.
